# Central Banks Search for Lessons From the Great Inflation Outbreak

## Context and Current Situation

After the most aggressive monetary-tightening campaign in four decades, central banks and economists are analyzing what could have prevented the cost-of-living crisis and how to avoid similar mistakes in the future. Markets are pricing in high-for-longer interest rates while a new conflict in the Middle East adds uncertainty to an already complex outlook for policymakers. Most major central banks including the Federal Reserve, Bank of England, Bank of Canada, European Central Bank, and Bank of Japan have raised rates significantly since January 2022.

## Three Central Policy Debates

The post-crisis policy analysis centers on three main questions: How much flexibility can central banks allow in reaching their 2% inflation targets, the effectiveness of quantitative easing in the policy toolkit, and the relationship between monetary and fiscal policy coordination.

### Rethinking Inflation Targets

Central banks are expected to allow more time for bringing inflation back to target if it reduces economic damage. Economists covering 16 of the world's most important central banks indicate policymakers will pursue targets with greater flexibility. A significant minority sees central banks accepting small deviations from the 2% target as long as inflation expectations remain anchored.

Some economists like former IMF chief Olivier Blanchard and former ECB Vice President Vitor Constancio argue for raising inflation targets themselves. However, this remains controversial and would only be credible after inflation returns to 2%. Bundesbank President Joachim Nagel notes that changing a goal you cannot achieve would be a mistake of the first order.

Global trends suggest inflation will remain stronger than pre-pandemic levels, with former Bank of England Governor Mark Carney among those believing rates won't return to historical lows. The IMF's Gita Gopinath emphasizes that policymakers should be ready to react preemptively to supply shocks rather than simply looking through them, as textbooks traditionally suggest.

The Bank for International Settlements argues there is room for greater tolerance of moderate and persistent shortfalls because low-inflation regimes have self-stabilizing properties, unlike high-inflation ones.

### Quantitative Easing and Asset Purchases

Trillions of dollars, euros, yen and pounds of asset purchases had little effect on prices in the face of global disinflationary forces until governments used the funds to inject cash into consumer pockets during Covid lockdowns. This has been blamed for distorting financial markets, with episodes like the Silicon Valley Bank collapse attributed partly to central bank reserves creation under QE.

Only 40% of economists predict central banks will use QE the same way as before. A quarter expect more sparing deployment, 30% see its sole future role as addressing financial stability concerns, and a small minority expects it won't be used again. The UK example illustrates another problem: the Bank of England required taxpayer indemnity for losses on its QE purchases, with estimates of over £200 billion in costs to the government over the next decade.

Bond-buying effectively swaps long-term borrowing costs for short-term ones. When interest rates were low, this was profitable for taxpayers, but now represents a disastrous trade. Central banks have limited experience unwinding their balance sheets, where small mistakes can trigger significant market turbulence, though recent quantitative tightening efforts have progressed smoothly as central banks are far from any squeeze thresholds.

The ECB faces additional legal constraints operating in a 20-country currency union, with concerns about illegally financing governments and debt mutualization already bringing court cases.

### Coordination Between Monetary and Fiscal Policy

Low interest rates and large-scale QE allowed governments to borrow cheaply for stimulus, protecting labor markets and consumers. However, spending blowouts throughout and after the pandemic contributed to the latest inflation outbreak. While restrained demand requires policy alignment, many governments fear tightening too hard will lead voters to replace them with populists or extremists.

Economists surveyed by Bloomberg predict fiscal policy will somewhat counteract the Federal Reserve's efforts to control inflation. Australian central bank governor Philip Lowe emphasized in his final speech that optimal policy would involve both monetary and fiscal roles with close coordination. However, Fed Chair Jerome Powell signaled the Federal Reserve is not ready to rely on such cooperation, stating our assignment is to deliver price stability regardless of fiscal policy stance.

ECB President Christine Lagarde acknowledged that working in tandem has proved helpful in some circumstances. Central bankers warn that failure to scale back fiscal spending risks higher interest rates. BIS general manager Agustin Carstens calls for a mindset change where growth depends less on fiscal and monetary policy and more on structural policies.

## Key Takeaways

According to Bloomberg's economist survey, central banks are unlikely to break their economies rushing to hit inflation targets, will use QE more sparingly in the future, and must contend with fiscal policy that may counter their monetary efforts. The path forward requires both flexibility in achieving targets and a reassessment of the tools and relationships that drive economic stability.
