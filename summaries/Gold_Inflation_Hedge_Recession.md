# Investing in Gold: Is Gold Still a Good Inflation Hedge in a Recession?

## Gold as a Historical Safe-Haven Asset

Gold has long been touted as the world's safe-haven metal, helping to protect investors against inflation and economic downturns. One of history's most enduring commodities, gold prices have been volatile this year following Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Europe's deepening energy crisis has resulted in a weaker euro, while increased US interest rates have strengthened the dollar, affecting gold spot prices on an almost daily basis. Despite these fluctuations, Bloomberg Intelligence expects central banks to continue viewing gold favorably as a reserve asset.

## Factors Affecting Gold Prices

Several factors influence gold prices, with inflation and interest rates being the most important and interconnected. Gold has an inherently limited supply, which makes it an inflation hedge. However, despite its reputation as a safe-haven investment, gold is not risk-free. Market uncertainty significantly influences gold prices, as evidenced during the pandemic when investors increased gold investments in bullion, stocks and exchange-traded funds as trillions of dollars were injected into the US economy through stimulus packages and interest rates reached record-low levels.

## Gold and Recessions

While gold has an inversely proportional relationship to inflation rates, it is less affected by recessions than many commodities. Gold is consistently in demand around the world, so a recession in any single region is unlikely to skew its international value. In a global recession, gold remains valuable because of its liquidity and accessibility as an asset to cash in when markets decline.

Due to its safe-haven reputation, gold tends to perform well during recessions. When the stock market collapsed in 2007, investment demand for gold spiked and the metal doubled in value between 2007 and 2011. Similarly, during the Covid-19 pandemic when fear and uncertainty reached all-time highs, gold prices hit record highs and inflows into gold-backed exchange-traded funds surged.

## The Complexity of Gold as a Safe Haven

The phrase safe haven does not always make sense when considering gold, which does not provide a yield. Gold can function as a safe haven in lower-interest-rate environments, but does not work as well when investors hold gold against the US dollar, which does produce a yield. Brian Gould, Head of Dealing at Capital.com, emphasizes this distinction.

When a central bank injects liquidity into the market to counter recession effects, inflation follows, lowering currency value and investor confidence in currency strength, which increases gold demand. Gold usually holds its value in weak economic environments. However, the current inflationary environment presents a different scenario. With inflation reaching extreme levels and central banks implementing rate hikes unseen in decades, gold's performance varies significantly by currency.

For example, gold reached new all-time highs in 2018 but has declined since measured against the US dollar. However, gold is 14% higher against the Japanese yen, 8.6% higher versus the pound, and 4.3% higher versus the euro. This demonstrates that gold can work as an inflation hedge, but not necessarily against the dollar. Gold often trades more like a risk asset than a traditional safe haven.

## Real Treasury Yields and Gold Relationships

Gold prices are linked to US Treasury real yields, representing net returns of expected inflation. When US real yields increase, gold's value decreases and vice versa. A higher real expected return on safe assets like US Treasury bonds discourages investment demand in non-yielding assets such as gold. When investors believe the Federal Reserve will continue raising interest rates aggressively and successfully lower inflation, this is not a favorable environment for gold, as Treasuries offer relatively more attractive returns.

However, when the market believes inflation cannot be significantly reduced even with rising interest rates, particularly if inflation stems from supply-side factors like energy crises over which central banks have limited control, gold returns to the forefront of investors' thinking. People seek safety in precious metals when concerned about losing real value from otherwise safe assets like cash and government bonds. These assets tend to decline when inflation expectations rise faster than nominal yields.

## Traditional Gold Investment Products

Historically, gold helps diversify multi-asset portfolios to smooth risk and return and reduce overall losses when stocks, bonds, real estate and other assets decline. Gold can be traded as physical gold, stocks, futures, contracts for differences (CFDs), and exchange-traded funds (ETFs).

Buying bullion bars or coins provides exposure to gold, but storage and insurance costs can be expensive. Spot prices have declined since reaching record highs following the Russian invasion of Ukraine. According to the World Gold Council, central banks are likely to maintain tight monetary policies, which lowers appeal for non-interest-bearing assets like gold jewelry for the remainder of 2022. Jewelry demand has slumped since 2021 following the strengthening dollar, making locally-purchased gold more expensive. China has experienced year-on-year declines in gold jewelry demand.

Physical gold provides flexibility to resell when needed, but no guarantee of receiving the same market price upon sale. Physical gold does not produce a yield while held, and profits from selling are subject to capital gains tax.

Holding gold mining company shares provides another exposure route, but performance depends on far more than just gold prices. Company reputation, production costs, reserves and exploration all factor into share performance alongside commodity price movements.

## Speculative Gold Trading

Investors interested in speculating on gold price movements rather than owning physical gold can gain exposure through multiple means. Gold CFDs are contracts that pay traders for the difference between entry and exit price if trade direction matches price movement. Investors benefit from upward changes by going long and can easily go short to benefit from downward changes.

Success depends on a trader's ability to analyze and predict market trends and understand factors affecting price movement. Futures, including CFDs, do not require full upfront funding, which may appeal to investors seeking leverage. However, futures contracts require management to maintain appropriate exposure levels, potentially suiting investors who prefer hands-on, long-term strategic approaches.

## Risk Management and Education

Investing in any security carries risk, and traders should carefully consider risks before trading gold as with any asset. CFD trading requires caution and is not for everyone. Trading with leverage necessitates understanding risk management principles. Educational resources and technology can help manage risks in gold trading. New traders entering the market need to understand that gold can move several percentage points and exhibits high liquidity at times.

While gold is a strong instrument that has stood the test of time for millennia, it can be very volatile. Understanding price movements in the commodity market is essential, especially as world economies enter uncertain periods. Investors benefit from education on how various factors affect gold, from macroeconomic policy shifts to currency fluctuations, before committing capital to gold investments.
